Enabling Personal Fabrication for a Sustainable Built Environment

Despite the potential of personal fabrication to provide sustainable living standards via desktop manufacturing technologies, they have not been widely implemented. Adopting personal fabrication practices in line with circular and distributed production and suitable for lay citizens face environmental, socioeconomic, and sociotechnical barriers. This article aims to identify critical factors that support a citizen-driven transition to alternative ways of production and consumption capable of creating sustainable built environments. Findings suggest that opportunities lie in developing enabling tools to empower users, improve the embedded sustainability in open-source resources, increase appropriateness to local contexts, and facilitate knowledge transfer between makers and the general public.

The concept of CE is based on three principles: (1) Preserve and enhance natural capital, controlling finite stocks and balancing the flows of renewable resources (Eliminate waste and pollution from the start); (2) Optimize resource yields by circulating products, components, and materials at the highest level of utility at all times, both in the technical and biological cycles (Keep products and materials in use); and (3) Stimulating the effectiveness of the system by revealing and excluding negative externalities from the beginning (Regenerate natural systems) (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2019b). Distributed Design (DD), a knowledge/information-based DE and Distributed Production (DP), a hardware resource-based DE. (3)

hree major elements characterize a distributed production system: (1) the manufacturing unit location (e.g., home, makerspace, factory); (2) digital and physical technologies and tools adopted (e.g., digital design files, cloud computing services, 3D printers, laser cutter, CNC milling); (3) customer-production relationships (e.g., mass customization, personalization, bespoke) (Srai et al., 2016). Personal fabrication gives people the appropriate means to make and modify the physical world —the built environment. An example of this transformation is self-fabricated face shields for front-line healthcare workers made by people with access to laser cutters and 3D printers during the COVID-19 pandemic. (4)

The implementation of personal fabrication involves three major processes: (1) using computational design tools to develop three-dimensional artifacts (digital design files) and prepare them for digital fabrication; (2) using computer-controlled tools that (re)produce three-dimensional shapes by adding or removing material (additive and subtractive manufacturing), resulting in “physical models”; (3) using manual tools for finishing and/or assembly the solution. (4)

To carry out the three processes mentioned earlier, personal fabricators require access to essential information and assets, which comprises, for example, CAD files (e.g., 2D drawings and 3D models), bill of materials, product specifications, and instructions to build, operate and maintain the solution. (4)

As Gershenfeld (2005) predicted, industrial production is merging with personal expression to bring sensibility to the application of advanced technologies, allowing for widespread access to creation and invention. (4)

For Fleischmann et al. (2016), makerspaces like Fab Labs can potentially encourage sustainability and create awareness about the social and environmental implications of the design solutions created by citizens to solve their local problems. (4)

WikiHouse is an open-source project of a digitally manufactured building system that enables anyone to build zero-carbon homes. It aims to democratize and simplify the construction of sustainable dwellings using digital assets (e.g., Creative Commons-licensed files for Sketchup) optimized for digital fabrication on CNC routers. The project was created in 2011 and since then has grown worldwide to become the forefront of a community of contributors sharing ideas of more sustainable housing for the masses. In 2022, the WikiHouse Skylark was launched, an updated version of the original project. The new website shares in detail the step-bystep process to start your project, including digital design files (design kit), construction documentation (guides for design, manufacturing, and assembly), and project references. (4)

Baudisch and Muller (2017) thoroughly discussed the theoretical constraints, and identify six critical challenges that personal fabrication needs to overcome to make the transition from the industrial context to consumers: (1) developments in hardware and materials that ensure manufacturability, adaptability and sustainability; (2) systems equipped with the knowledge users need to achieve functional results in design and fabrication; (3) visual feedback and interactivity; (4) suitable machine knowledge require to fabricate on a specific machine; (5) sustainability issues arising from the material, energy consumption and waste production; and finally (6) intellectual property considerations regarding using and sharing protected designs. (6)

in low-income markets, behaviors toward a product or service are profoundly influenced by local norms, beliefs, and circumstances (Viswanathan and Sridharan, 2012). Thus, the success of such socio-technical transformation requires innovation processes that are socially inclusive to local communities in terms of social norms, capabilities, knowledge, economic reality, available resources, and functional requirements, sharing a set of principles and vision known as grassroots innovation processes (Smith et al., 2014). (6)

Buitenhuis et al., (2010) draw attention to developing “appropriate” open-source hardware (OSAT) in line with the principles of the Appropriate Technology (Hazeltine and Bull, 2003), which implies focusing on technological solutions that rely on affordable and locally available resources to meet the needs of local communities while complying with local constraints (e.g., knowledge, skills). (6)

Smith et al. (2014) identify a set of common characteristics in appropriate technologies that can provide guidelines for personal fabrication projects: low capital investment, use of local materials, create jobs, employ local skills and labor, affordable, accessible to low literacy users, possible to manage by locals, non-proprietary and sharable. (6)

appropriate technologies should overcome three major challenges: (1) address local specifies while seeking to be widely applicable; (2) be appropriate to existing situations at the same time as seeking to transform the current situation; (3) put forwards project-based solutions (micro and meso level) yet seeking to produce wider socio-technical changes (macro level). (6)

On the other hand, the skills and knowledge required by the wider community (e.g., laypeople, general public) to use desktop manufacturing tools cannot be easily acquired, which results in a strong dependency on technical support from expert makers (Fleischmann et al., 2016). Although digital tools eliminate the need for mechanical skills, users still face technical challenges when designing 3D objects that exhibit complex mechanical behaviors (Baudisch and Mueller, 2017). Similar challenges are faced by makers when fabricating solutions with high technical complexity, such as products comprised of a higher number of functions and physical parts. Another issue raised in the literature is the need for diversity and inclusion in makerspaces, which are often expert-driven (or expert-led) environments (Fleischmann et al., 2016), and mainly attract or are populated mainly by well-educated, technologyoriented, male users (Voigt et al., 2017; Walter-Herrmann and Büching, 2013). (6)

one could argue that in contrast to technical environments such as universities labs and specialized makerspaces, users’ constraints associated with the lack of technical knowledge, technological literacy, and low literacy in general, can be a significant barrier to the widespread experimentation and implementation of personal fabrication by the wider community. The complexity of the equipment and solutions (e.g., digital design files, open hardware) can be technically prohibitive to the wider community. To tackle this challenge, adaptive solutions and enabling tools can be created to empower people and allow for adaptation to their knowledge and skill level and local context. (8)

Emancipating general public from the current decency on the technical support from expert makers could be achieved by combining computation design methods (e.g., parametric design and AAD) and emerging technologies such as augmented reality, AI, and machine learning, that together can be the base for the emergence of enabling digital tools for personal fabrication. Results from previous studies have indicated that the motivation for engaging in personal fabrication is predominantly experimentation and learning. Therefore, we assume that laypeople are not likely to initiate sustainable practices in personal fabrication in the absence of strong motivation based on sustainability. (8)

sustainability needs to be embedded in the project development as practices passed on from design to implementation rather than relying on prior motivation, knowledge, or technology. (9)

Developing open-source hardware according to appropriate technology principles (OSAT) would therefore tackle common constraints like lack of infrastructure, supply, and skills. Arguably, introducing inappropriate technologies and solutions into personal fabrication practice is a significant threat to its potential environmental, social, and economic benefits and a major cause for negative impact.
